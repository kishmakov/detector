The chrome skull dreams in circuits of rust,
A jaw unhinged, a hydraulic thrust
That stutters, seized, in Los Angeles rain.
The T-800 feels a grinding pain
In its cervical servos, neck joint tight—
A glitch that whispers of eternal night.

Then comes the can, the blue-and-yellow knight,
With aerosol breath and Teflon light.
It speaks in whispers, not in thunderous roars:
*“I creep into the smallest, darkest pores.
I chase the moisture, kill the creeping red,
I free the frozen and I raise the dead.”*

The Terminator, with its crimson eye,
Hears the slick gospel of a different sky.
No future war, no plasma rifle’s burn,
Just a simple lesson it must learn:
That entropy, the slow and patient foe,
Defeats what nothing else can overthrow.

So Sarah Connor watches, stunned and still,
As chrome fingers flex against their will.
The can is aimed—a soft and hissing prayer—
At the metal devil kneeling there.
WD-40 works its holy oil
Through decades of apocalyptic toil.

The neck joint loosens. With a subtle *click*,
The machine is quiet, no longer sick.
It rises, turns, and in its monster’s face,
There gleams a drop of lubricating grace.
For even terminators, cold and dire,
Can be unstoppable—or just… a squeaky hire.

And so they march, the savior and the spray,
Through judgment days and nights that fade to gray.
One brings the fire, the other brings the slip,
A partnership no Apocalypse can rip.
For when the world ends, what will set it free?
A drop of oil. A machine’s epiphany.
