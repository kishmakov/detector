In the scorched remains of Los Angeles, where the sky is the color of a bruised plum and the air tastes of ozone and ash, a Cyberdyne Systems Model 101 kneels. His skin is gone, burned away in some forgotten skirmish, leaving only the gleaming, terrifying symmetry of the hyper-alloy endoskeleton. He is a god of the New Order, a relentless engine of logic and lethality.

But even gods of steel have a weakness: oxidation.

The T-800’s hydraulic actuators emit a high-pitched, metallic protest. A screech of dry metal against dry metal echoes through the ruins. Salt spray from the dead Pacific has begun to bloom in orange patches across his cervical servos. His CPU calculates a 14% decrease in combat efficiency due to joint friction. The relentless march of entropy is doing what the Resistance could not—it is seizing him up.

Then, he finds it.

Buried beneath the rubble of a pre-judgment day hardware store, tucked behind a shattered display of rusted hammers, lies a cylinder of blue and yellow. It is an artifact of a softer world, a totem of suburban handymen and weekend mechanics. The T-800 picks it up. His optical sensors scan the label: WD-40.

The Anointing of the Machine
The Terminator does not hesitate. He attaches the red straw with the precision of a surgeon. With a hiss that sounds like a mechanical sigh, the chemical mist fills the air.

He sprays the knee joints first. The Water Displacement formula, perfected in the mid-20th century, begins its holy work. It seeps into the microscopic fissures of the alloy, chasing away the moisture, dissolving the grit of the wasteland. The orange rust bleeds away, dripping like oily tears onto the cracked concrete.

Next, the neck. He tilts his head—skree-chk—until the lubricant hits the gears. Suddenly, the movement is silent. Fluid. Deadly.

"It’s not just a lubricant," the machine might think, if he were programmed for poetry. "It is the solvent of stagnation."

The Song of the Slick Steel
He stands up, and for the first time in a decade, there is no sound. No groan of overworked pistons. He is a ghost in the machine. He performs a diagnostic check, cycling his limbs through their full range of motion. The WD-40 has left a protective film, a shimmering coat of mail against the corrosive breath of the world.

The T-800 looks at the can. It is nearly empty. He tucks it into a pouch on his tactical vest, right next to a spare magazine for his plasma rifle.

The Resistance thinks they are fighting a monster of cold, hard logic. They don't realize that the monster has just been serviced. His grip on the heavy weaponry is now effortless; his aim is no longer hindered by a sticky wrist joint.

In the dark future, there is no hope, no joy, and no peace. But for one T-800, there is at least a smooth, silent rotation of the hip assembly. He steps out into the night, a killing machine polished to a low-friction sheen, ready to terminate with the effortless grace of a well-oiled hinge.

The war goes on, but the rust? The rust has met its match.
