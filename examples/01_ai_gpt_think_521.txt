In the blue dawn of the workshop, when the metal sweats its cold,
and the rails keep their small complaints like secrets left untold,
there comes a humble alchemy in a can of golden spray—
WD-40 humming softly through the morning’s rusted gray.

It kisses hinges, turns the reluctant wheel,
loosens the stubborn silence iron learns to feel.
A friend to squeaks and seized-up threads, to bolts that forgot their song,
it slides through the language of friction, and teaches things to move along.

Then from the ash of future wars, through smoke and chrome and fire,
steps the T-800, steady as a machine’s own prayer,
a titan built for judgment, with a frame of iron law,
its red eye measuring the world with a patient, merciless awe.

Not soft as mercy, not warm as rain,
but forged in the furnace where purpose outlives pain.
It knows the weight of footsteps, the geometry of fear,
the sharp bright edge between the now and what is coming near.

And what is WD-40 to such a sentinel of steel?
A small bright miracle, perhaps, a tool that helps the wheel
turn without struggle, a quiet trick of human hands,
a drop of grace on frozen joints, on jammed and sulking bands.

It does not command armies. It does not bend time or bone.
It asks for no prophecy, no throne of chrome, no throne of stone.
It enters with a whisper where brute force would only fail,
and leaves behind a smoothness like wind sliding down a rail.

Imagine then the meeting:
not war, not chase, not the flash of a final scene,
but a garage at midnight, lit by one bare fluorescent beam,
the T-800 standing motionless beside a workbench scarred and old,
while a mechanic, grinning, shakes the can of liquid gold.

A hiss like tiny summer, a shimmer on the dark,
a silver chain released from its long and stubborn bark.
The machine tilts its head, as if learning a new kind of creed:
that even iron soldiers sometimes benefit from speed.

For every future built on gears,
for every present clogged with years,
there is a lesson in the balance of these two:
one made to endure all damage, one made to undo
the little failures that collect in hinges, locks, and fate—
the grand and the practical both waiting at the gate.

The T-800 walks through ruins with a purpose carved in flame.
WD-40 travels through the small wars,
quietly changing the game.
One is a legend of relentless, relentless machine-born might;
the other, a can of practiced mercy that restores the night.

And somewhere between the apocalypse and a backyard repair,
between a cyborg’s iron gaze and the scent of engine air,
there is the human instinct to fix what’s broken, to keep moving on—
to face the stubborn world and make it sing before it’s gone.

So let the future bring its thunder, let the gears of doom align;
let the chrome of destiny reflect the workshop light.
We will still reach for something simple, bright, and true:
a can of WD-40, and the unblinking T-800.
